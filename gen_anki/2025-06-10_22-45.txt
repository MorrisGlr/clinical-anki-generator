1. A 27-year-old woman comes to the physician for a routine examination. She recently immigrated to the USA from Haiti. She feels well and is asymptomatic. She has a 6-year history of type 2 diabetes mellitus. Treatment with metformin was begun 3 weeks ago. She received all recommended vaccinations during childhood, including BCG vaccine 20 years ago. Her pulse is 74/min, respirations are 12/min, and blood pressure is 104/72 mm Hg. The remainder of the examination shows no abnormalities. Her fingerstick blood glucose concentration is 168 mg/dl. A PPD skin test produces 12 mm of induration at 48 hours. An x-ray of the chest shows no abnormalities. Which of the following is the most appropriate next step in management?	Correct answer: C</br></br>a. Repeat PPD skin test in 6 months</br>b. Repeat x-ray of the chest in 3 months</br>c. Begin isoniazid therapy</br>d. Begin isoniazid therapy only if the patient becomes symptomatic</br>e. Begin isoniazid, rifampin, and pyrazinamide therapy</br></br></br><p>Correct Answer Selection<br /></br>c. Begin isoniazid therapy (plus pyridoxine) for latent tuberculosis infection (LTBI)</p></br><p>─────────────────────────────────────────<br /></br>Vignette Analysis<br /></br>• Recent immigrant from Haiti – a country with high TB prevalence.<br /></br>• Immigration occurred “recently” (&lt; 5 years) → PPD cut-off for a positive test is ≥ 10 mm.<br /></br>• PPD induration = 12 mm → meets the ≥ 10 mm threshold.<br /></br>• Completely asymptomatic + normal chest x-ray → rules out active pulmonary TB and points toward latent infection.<br /></br>• Prior BCG vaccination 20 years ago does NOT change interpretation once ≥ 10 mm in a high-risk group is reached.</p></br><p>Pattern: Positive screening test (≥ 10 mm) in a high-risk asymptomatic patient with normal CXR = latent TB → treat with INH.</p></br><p>─────────────────────────────────────────<br /></br>Clinical Critical Reasoning<br /></br>Step-wise approach to a PPD result:<br /></br>1. Determine the appropriate induration cut-off (5, 10, or 15 mm).<br /></br>2. If above the cut-off, rule out active disease with history + CXR.<br /></br>3. If no evidence of active disease → treat LTBI with INH for 6–9 months (plus daily pyridoxine).<br /></br>Key pitfall avoided: “BCG vaccination” does NOT override standard interpretation; do not ignore a clearly positive test.</p></br><p>Core takeaway: All patients with a positive TB screening test at the appropriate cut-off and a negative evaluation for active disease should receive INH for LTBI to prevent reactivation.</p></br><p>─────────────────────────────────────────<br /></br>Pathophysiology Review<br /></br>• Mycobacterium tuberculosis may remain dormant in granulomas (latent infection).<br /></br>• Risk of future reactivation is 5–10% over a lifetime; higher in diabetics, recent immigrants, HIV, etc.<br /></br>• INH (inhibits mycolic-acid synthesis) eradicates or markedly lowers bacillary burden, reducing reactivation risk by ~90%.</p></br><p>─────────────────────────────────────────<br /></br>Treatment &amp; Diagnostics<br /></br>• LTBI: Isoniazid 300 mg PO daily for 9 months (or 6 months if adherence is an issue) + pyridoxine 25–50 mg daily to prevent neuropathy.<br /></br>• Baseline LFTs only if liver disease or heavy alcohol use; otherwise monitor clinically.<br /></br>• Alternative regimens (e.g., rifampin daily x 4 mo) are used when INH is contraindicated/intolerant.</p></br><p>─────────────────────────────────────────<br /></br>Incorrect Answer Analysis<br /></br>a. Repeat PPD in 6 months<br /></br>   – PPD conversion takes ≥ 3 weeks; waiting offers no benefit once the test is already positive at the correct cut-off.  </p></br><p>b. Repeat chest x-ray in 3 months<br /></br>   – No evidence of active disease now; repeating imaging does not treat latent infection or prevent reactivation.  </p></br><p>d. Begin INH only if patient becomes symptomatic<br /></br>   – Defers preventive therapy; misses window to prevent future reactivation.  </p></br><p>e. Start INH, rifampin, pyrazinamide (triple therapy)<br /></br>   – Multidrug regimen is for active TB or certain special situations (e.g., TB meningitis). Normal CXR &amp; no symptoms = latent, not active disease; adding unnecessary drugs increases toxicity risk.</p></br><p>─────────────────────────────────────────<br /></br>Bottom Line<br /></br>A 12-mm induration in a recent immigrant qualifies as a positive PPD. With a normal chest x-ray and no symptoms, the patient has latent TB infection and should begin isoniazid therapy with pyridoxine to prevent future reactivation.</p>
2. A previously healthy 7-year-old boy is brought to the physician by his father because of a 1-day history of right hip pain and an associated limp. Five days ago, he fell off his bed while wrestling with his 5-year-old brother. Three weeks ago, he had a cold that resolved within 5 days. He takes no medications. Immunizations are up-to-date. He is at the 50th percentile for height and weight. His temperature is 37.6 C 99.7 F, pulse is 100/min, respirations are 18/min, and blood pressure is 98/66 mm Hg. He is alert and in no distress. Examination of the hips and knees shows no swelling, warmth, or em,thema. Passive motion of the right hip produces moderate pain. Laboratory studies show:</br>Leukocyte count</br>Segmented neutrophils</br>Bands</br>Eosinophils</br>Lymphocytes</br>Monocytes</br>Erythrocyte sedimentation rate</br>Serum</br>C-reactive protein</br>Antinuclear antibody</br>10,000/mm3</br>52 percent</br>3 percent</br>3 percent</br>37 percent</br>5 percent</br>24 mm/h</br>3000 μg/L N=67-1800</br>negative</br>X-rays of the right hip show no abnormalities. Ultrasonography of the right hip shows mild joint effusion. Needle aspiration of the right hip joint yields clear fluid; a Gram stain of the fluid shows no organisms. Which of the</br>following is the most likely long-term complication of this patient's current condition?	Correct answer: E</br></br>a. Hip flexion contracture</br>b. Leg length discrepancy</br>c. Pauciarticular arthritis</br>d. Polyarticular arthritis</br>e. There are no long-term complications</br></br></br><p>Correct Answer Selection<br /></br>e. There are no long-term complications  </p></br><p>──────────────────────────────<br /></br>Vignette Analysis<br /></br>• Age (7 yr) + acute (1-day) atraumatic hip pain and limp.<br /></br>• Low-grade or no fever (99.7 °F).<br /></br>• Normal leukocyte count, mild ESR elevation (24 mm/h), modest CRP rise.<br /></br>• Hip US: small effusion; aspirate = clear, Gram stain negative.<br /></br>• Recent viral URI (3 wk ago) – classic antecedent trigger.  </p></br><p>These findings match transient (toxic) synovitis, the most common cause of acute hip pain in children 3–10 yr. Septic arthritis is ruled out by: afebrile/low fever, normal WBC, ESR &lt;40, clear aspirate, negative Gram stain.  </p></br><p>──────────────────────────────<br /></br>Clinical Critical Reasoning<br /></br>Kocher criteria help distinguish transient synovitis from septic arthritis (fever &gt;38.5 °C, WBC &gt;12 000, ESR &gt;40, CRP &gt;2 mg/dL, inability to bear weight). This patient meets only one criterion (CRP borderline), giving &lt;2% probability of septic arthritis. Transient synovitis is self-limited (resolves within 1–4 wk) and does not damage cartilage or bone; therefore no chronic sequelae are expected. Core takeaway: recognize transient synovitis and know that it is benign with full recovery.  </p></br><p>──────────────────────────────<br /></br>Pathophysiology Review<br /></br>• Etiology: Post-viral or post-traumatic lymphocytic inflammation of the synovium.<br /></br>• Mechanism: Synovial effusion causes pain with passive ROM but does not infect or ischemically injure the femoral head.<br /></br>• Natural history: Symptoms peak over 24–48 h, resolve with NSAIDs and limited weight-bearing; radiographs remain normal.  </p></br><p>──────────────────────────────<br /></br>Treatment &amp; Diagnostics<br /></br>• Diagnostics already done: hip US (effusion), joint tap (non-purulent, culture-negative).<br /></br>• Management: NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen), rest, early mobilization as tolerated; no antibiotics or surgery needed.<br /></br>• Follow-up: Reassess in 1–2 wk to ensure resolution. No imaging follow-up required if symptoms resolve.  </p></br><p>──────────────────────────────<br /></br>Incorrect Answer Analysis  </p></br><p>a. Hip flexion contracture<br /></br>– Seen in slipped capital femoral epiphysis or advanced Legg-Calvé-Perthes due to chronic deformity. Would show abnormal X-ray (posteriorly displaced epiphysis or collapsed femoral head). Our patient’s X-ray is normal and course is acute.  </p></br><p>b. Leg length discrepancy<br /></br>– Complication of Legg-Calvé-Perthes (avascular necrosis of femoral head) or chronic JIA affecting growth plates. That condition presents with insidious, painless limp in boys 4–10 yr, limited abduction/internal rotation, normal labs. NOT an acute painful hip with effusion.  </p></br><p>c. Pauciarticular arthritis<br /></br>– A subtype of juvenile idiopathic arthritis (≤4 joints in first 6 mo). Presents with &gt;6-wk chronic joint swelling, morning stiffness, positive ANA, risk of uveitis. Labs often show elevated ESR; antecedent viral illness not typical. Our patient’s ANA is negative and symptom duration is 1 day.  </p></br><p>d. Polyarticular arthritis<br /></br>– Another JIA subtype involving ≥5 joints, symmetric, chronic course, often RF positive. Again, presentation is chronic, not acute, and would not resolve spontaneously.  </p></br><p>e. There are no long-term complications (Correct)<br /></br>– Transient synovitis is entirely self-limited; children regain full hip function with no residual structural or functional deficits.  </p></br><p>──────────────────────────────<br /></br>Key Takeaway<br /></br>In a child with acute hip pain, mild or absent systemic signs, normal labs, clear joint fluid, and recent viral illness, think transient synovitis—treat conservatively and reassure families that it carries no long-term sequelae.</p>
3. A 37-year-old primigravid woman at 12 weeks' gestation comes to the physician because of a 1-day history of urinary frequency and moderate pain and burning with urination. She has not had fever, abdominal or flank pain, or abnormal vaginal discharge. During the past year, she has had five urinary tract infections that began within 3 days of having sexual intercourse; each episode resolved after 1 day of treatment with ciprofloxacin or trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole. She has no history of serious illness and takes no medications. She is sexually active with one male partner. Vital signs are within normal limits. Physical examination, including pelvic examination, shows no abnormalities. Urinalysis shows numerous WBCs and bacteria. The patient is prescribed a 7-day course of nitrofurantoin. Which of the following is the most appropriate next step in management for the remainder of this patient's pregnancy?	Correct answer: C</br></br>a. Observation only</br>b. Postcoital ciprofloxacin therapy</br>c. Postcoital nitrofurantoin therapy</br>d. Daily ciprofloxacin therapy</br>e. Daily levofloxacin therapy</br></br></br><p>Correct Answer Selection<br /></br>a. Observation only  </p></br><p>Vignette Analysis<br /></br>• “12 weeks’ gestation” – first-trimester pregnancy; drug safety is paramount.<br /></br>• “Numerous WBCs and bacteria” + dysuria/urgency → acute uncomplicated cystitis.<br /></br>• “5 UTIs in the past year that began within 3 days of intercourse” – history of recurrent post-coital cystitis BEFORE pregnancy, not during pregnancy.<br /></br>• The episode is being treated appropriately with a pregnancy-safe 7-day nitrofurantoin course (avoid fluoroquinolones &amp; TMP-SMX in 1st trimester).<br /></br>Key distinction: Prior recurrences occurred outside of pregnancy, so she does not yet meet criteria for prophylaxis DURING pregnancy.</p></br><p>Clinical Critical Reasoning<br /></br>Step 1: Treat the current infection with a pregnancy-safe agent (nitrofurantoin 100 mg PO BID x 5-7 days).<br /></br>Step 2: Test-of-cure urine culture 1 week after therapy and then routine screening urine culture each trimester (because all pregnant women are screened for asymptomatic bacteriuria).<br /></br>Step 3: Prophylactic antibiotics (post-coital or daily) are reserved only if the patient develops ≥2 symptomatic UTIs while pregnant or has persistent bacteriuria despite treatment. Until that happens, observation is sufficient.</p></br><p>Core takeaway: A history of recurrent cystitis before conception is NOT an automatic indication for suppressive antibiotics in pregnancy; manage current infection, confirm eradication, and observe.</p></br><p>Pathophysiology Review<br /></br>• Pregnancy → progesterone &amp; uterine enlargement cause ureteral dilation, urinary stasis → ↑ risk of bacteriuria progressing to pyelonephritis, hence universal screening.<br /></br>• Nitrofurantoin concentrates in urine, minimal systemic effects, generally safe except near term (risk of neonatal hemolysis in G6PD deficiency).<br /></br>• Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin) risk fetal cartilage &amp; bone damage; avoid throughout pregnancy.<br /></br>• TMP-SMX: risk of congenital anomalies when used in first trimester (folate antagonist) and near term (kernicterus).</p></br><p>Treatments &amp; Diagnostic Tests Review<br /></br>Initial test: urine dipstick/urinalysis ± culture.<br /></br>Treatment choices for cystitis in pregnancy:<br /></br>  – Nitrofurantoin 5–7 days (avoid after 38 weeks)<br /></br>  – Amoxicillin-clavulanate 3–7 days<br /></br>  – Cephalexin 5–7 days<br /></br>Test-of-cure culture 1 week after completion.<br /></br>Suppressive therapy (e.g., nitrofurantoin 100 mg nightly) ONLY if two or more infections during the same pregnancy.</p></br><p>Incorrect Answer Analysis<br /></br>b. Post-coital ciprofloxacin therapy<br /></br>• Fluoroquinolone is teratogenic; contraindicated in pregnancy.<br /></br>• Prophylaxis not indicated until recurrent infections occur DURING pregnancy.</p></br><p>c. Post-coital nitrofurantoin therapy<br /></br>• Nitrofurantoin is safe, but prophylaxis is premature; guidelines recommend initiating only after ≥2 symptomatic UTIs in the current pregnancy.</p></br><p>d. Daily ciprofloxacin therapy<br /></br>• Same teratogenic concerns as (b) plus unnecessary broad coverage; not recommended in any trimester.</p></br><p>e. Daily levofloxacin therapy<br /></br>• Another fluoroquinolone; same fetal toxicity and over-treatment issues.</p></br><p>Common Pitfall: Automatically extending a prepregnancy “recurrent UTI” regimen into pregnancy. Instead, treat the infection, document cure, and observe; institute prophylaxis only if the woman starts having recurrent episodes while pregnant.</p></br><p>Bottom line: In a pregnant patient successfully treated for her first cystitis of the pregnancy, the correct next step is routine follow-up and observation, not antibiotic prophylaxis.</p>
4. An otherwise healthy 12-year-old boy is brought to the emergency department 4 hours after the sudden onset of severe pain in his right testis. There is no history of trauma. Vital signs are within normal limits. On examination, the right testis is palpated in the upper hemiscrotum; it is exquisitely tender and twice the size of the left testis, which is normal. The remainder of the examination shows no abnormalities. Results of a complete blood count are within the reference ranges, and urinalysis shows no abnormalities. In addition to administration of morphine, which of the following is the most appropriate next step in management?	Correct answer: E</br></br>a. Reexamination in 4 hours</br>b. Technetium 99m scan of the right testis</br>c. Intravenous administration of antibiotics</br>d. Reduction of the scrotal mass</br>e. Emergency orchidopexy</br></br></br><p>Correct Answer Selection<br /></br>e. Emergency orchiopexy (scrotal exploration with detorsion of the affected testis and fixation of both testes)</p></br><p>────────────────────────────────────────</br>Vignette Analysis<br /></br>• “Sudden onset of severe pain” in an adolescent male → classic presentation of testicular torsion.<br /></br>• Testis “palpated in the upper hemiscrotum … exquisitely tender and twice the size of the left” → high-riding, enlarged, edematous testis after cord twisting.<br /></br>• Normal vitals, normal UA, normal CBC → rules against infection (epididymitis/orchitis) or systemic illness.<br /></br>• No trauma history → favors torsion over hematocele or rupture.<br /></br>Pattern: adolescent + abrupt unilateral pain + high-riding/swollen testis = testicular torsion until proven otherwise.</p></br><p>────────────────────────────────────────</br>Clinical Critical Reasoning<br /></br>Testicular torsion is a time-critical surgical emergency. Testicular viability rapidly declines after 6 hours of ischemia (≈90 % salvage &lt;6 h, &lt;20 % after 12 h). Therefore, any delay for imaging or observation risks irreversible infarction. Definitive management is immediate scrotal exploration, surgical detorsion, and bilateral orchiopexy (to prevent future torsion on the contralateral side, which typically has the same “bell-clapper” anatomic defect).</p></br><p>Core takeaway: When torsion is strongly suspected clinically, skip confirmatory tests and go straight to the OR.</p></br><p>────────────────────────────────────────</br>Pathophysiology Review<br /></br>• Etiology: Congenital “bell-clapper” deformity → tunica vaginalis attaches high on spermatic cord, allowing free rotation.<br /></br>• Mechanism: Spermatic cord twists → venous outflow obstruction → edema → arterial inflow compromise → ischemia/necrosis.<br /></br>• Clinical manifestations: sudden severe pain, high-riding/horizontally lying testis, absent cremasteric reflex, N/V. UA usually normal.</p></br><p>────────────────────────────────────────</br>Treatment &amp; Diagnostic Tests<br /></br>First-line: Emergent surgical detorsion + bilateral orchiopexy.<br /></br>Manual detorsion (“opening a book”) may be attempted only if OR unavailable immediately, but does not replace surgery.<br /></br>Doppler US or radionuclide scan can confirm absent flow, but only if diagnosis is uncertain and results will not delay surgery.</p></br><p>────────────────────────────────────────</br>Incorrect Answer Analysis<br /></br>a. Re-examination in 4 hours<br /></br>   • Delays definitive management; salvage rates plummet with time. Observation appropriate only when torsion has been confidently excluded.</p></br><p>b. Technetium-99m scan of the right testis<br /></br>   • Sensitive for absent perfusion, but waiting for imaging costs precious time. Imaging is for equivocal cases, not when classic findings are present.</p></br><p>c. Intravenous antibiotics<br /></br>   • Would be correct for acute epididymitis/orchitis (fever, dysuria, pyuria, preserved cremasteric reflex, pain relieved with elevation—Prehn sign). None are present here.</p></br><p>d. Reduction of the scrotal mass (manual detorsion only)<br /></br>   • May temporarily relieve pain but is unreliable, may retorse, and does not address contralateral risk. Even after successful bedside detorsion, urgent surgical fixation is still required—so definitive step is still emergency orchiopexy.</p></br><p>────────────────────────────────────────</br>Bottom Line<br /></br>In a child or adolescent with sudden severe unilateral testicular pain and a high-riding tender testis, presume torsion and take the patient straight to the OR for emergent detorsion and bilateral orchiopexy. Time = testicle.</p>
5. A 56-year-old woman with recurrent ovarian cancer is admitted to the hospital for management of inoperable small-bowel obstruction. Despite undergoing four courses of chemotherapy during the past 5 years, the cancer continues to progress. She currently takes no medications. A nasogastric tube is placed. Her temperature is 37 C 98.6  F, pulse is 80/min, respirations are 12/min, and blood pressure is 120/70 mm Hg. Abdominal examination shows distention and decreased bowel sounds. Family meeting to determine goals of care</br>c. lleostomy</br>d. Intravenous total parenteral nutrition</br>e. Placement of a percutaneous gastrostomy tube</br></br></br><p>Correct Answer Selection<br /></br>B. Family meeting to determine goals of care  </p></br><p>───────────────────────────<br /></br>Vignette Analysis<br /></br>• “Recurrent ovarian cancer … continues to progress despite four courses of chemotherapy” ‒ terminal, chemoresistant disease.<br /></br>• “Inoperable small-bowel obstruction” ‒ no curative surgical option; likely malignant bowel obstruction, a common end-of-life event in ovarian cancer.<br /></br>• No active medications and stable vitals: She is not acutely crashing; immediate life-saving intervention isn’t required.<br /></br>→ The clinical picture screams limited prognosis and high symptom burden, so the next priority is clarifying what the patient actually wants before any further invasive steps are taken.</p></br><p>Clinical Critical Reasoning<br /></br>Step 1: Recognize that she has a terminal condition with a new serious complication.<br /></br>Step 2: Before initiating additional procedures (TPN, PEG, surgery) or discharging her, first make sure she understands the prognosis and articulate her values and preferences.<br /></br>Step 3: A structured family (or goals-of-care) meeting fulfills this need and is therefore the most appropriate NEXT step.</p></br><p>Core takeaway: In a patient with terminal malignancy and new life-limiting complication, the first action is to clarify goals of care through a patient/family meeting before pursuing any further treatments or disposition.</p></br><p>───────────────────────────<br /></br>Pathophysiology Review (High-Yield)<br /></br>• Malignant bowel obstruction in ovarian cancer results from peritoneal carcinomatosis, tumor infiltration, or adhesions.<br /></br>• Median survival once obstruction is inoperable is measured in weeks to a few months.<br /></br>• Interventions such as TPN or surgery rarely change survival and often worsen quality of life.</p></br><p>───────────────────────────<br /></br>Treatments &amp; Diagnostic Tests Review<br /></br>After a goals-of-care discussion, management usually shifts to:<br /></br>• Symptom control (opioids for pain, antiemetics, octreotide to reduce secretions, dexamethasone to decrease edema).<br /></br>• Possible NG decompression for comfort if tolerated.<br /></br>• Hospice enrollment if patient chooses comfort-focused care.<br /></br>• Nutritional support is generally NOT recommended; Ileostomy<br /></br>• Requires laparotomy; morbidity is high, benefit minimal because the obstruction is “inoperable” (diffuse carcinomatosis). Not offered without discussion of prognosis.</p></br><p>D.
